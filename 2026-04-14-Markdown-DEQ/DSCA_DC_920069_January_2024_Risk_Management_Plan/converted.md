## 1.0 Introduction

Hart & Hickman, PC (H&H) has prepared this Risk Management Plan (RMP) for the Galaxy Cleaners site on behalf of the North Carolina Dry-Cleaning Solvent Cleanup Act (DSCA) Program. The former Galaxy Cleaners facility is located at 101-B New Waverly Place in Cary, Wake County, North Carolina. Site assessment activities have confirmed that soil and groundwater contamination associated with the Galaxy Cleaners site is confined to the source property and one off-source property. This RMP is intended to comply with the requirements of DSCA (N.C.G.S. 143-215.104A *et seqs*) and promulgated rules and follows the outline provided in the DSCA Program’s risk-based corrective action (RBCA) guidance. A site location map is included as Figure 1.

## 2.0 Objectives of RMP

Assessment activities completed at the Galaxy Cleaners site indicated that the dry-cleaning solvent tetracholorethylene (PCE) is present above Title 15A NCAC 2L .0202 Groundwater Standards (2L Standards) on the source property and one off-source property. Bromodichloromethane is also present above 2L Standards on the source property. The off-source property with PCE in groundwater is:

- 530 New Waverly Place, Cary, NC, Parcel ID: 0762635240, Oaks Development LLC (owner), multi-unit offices (tenant).

H&H submitted a Risk Assessment report for the site on April 30, 2015. The results of the risk evaluation indicate no exceedances of acceptable risk levels under both the current and future scenarios for the source property and off-source property. However, the evaluation was based on specific land use conditions that require an RMP. Thus, the objective of this RMP is to ensure that those land use conditions remain valid in the future.

## 3.0 Summary of Approved Risk Assessment Report

Based on the assessment results for the site, H&H completed a Risk Assessment report dated April 30, 2015. The first step in the risk assessment process is development of an exposure model. Two exposure units were assigned: Exposure Unit #1 encompassing the former Galaxy Cleaners facility and the remaining area of the contaminant plume on the source property, and Exposure Unit #2 encompassing the extent of the groundwater contaminant plume located beyond the boundaries of the source property.

Complete exposure pathways identified for Exposure Unit #1 include indoor inhalation of vapor emissions, outdoor inhalation of vapor emissions, and surficial soil exposure by a current or future non-residential worker, future resident, or construction worker. H&H conservatively used the maximum concentrations detected in soil, groundwater, and sub-slab vapor within the exposure unit as representative concentrations (RCs) for the risk evaluation. Although indoor air sampling was previously conducted in the former dry-cleaning facility, the indoor air data was not used because the former dry-cleaning space was subsequently upfitted and incorporated into the adjacent grocery store space. Thus, the sub-slab vapor data was used to evaluate both current and future indoor air risk exposure. The indoor inhalation of vapor pathway was evaluated using the DSCA risk calculator. The remaining pathways were evaluated using soil and groundwater data and the Groundwater Services, Inc. (GSI) risk software. The results of the modeling under the current and future scenarios indicated no exceedances of acceptable risk levels for a non-residential worker, resident, or construction worker.

Complete exposure pathways identified for Exposure Unit #2 include indoor and outdoor inhalation of vapor emissions from groundwater. H&H conservatively evaluated a residential scenario to minimize land use restrictions on the property, even though the property is used for non-residential purposes. H&H also conservatively used the maximum groundwater contaminant concentrations detected within the exposure unit as the RCs for groundwater. The outdoor air exposure pathway was modeled using the GSI risk software, and the indoor air exposure pathway was modeled using the DSCA risk calculator. The results of the modeling under the current and future scenarios indicated no exceedances of acceptable risk levels for Exposure Unit #2.

As part of the risk assessment, H&H also evaluated the protection of groundwater use and surface water pathways. For the protection of groundwater use evaluation, H&H identified the nearest potential point of exposure (POE) as the closest downgradient property boundary where groundwater impacts have not been observed. Modeling under this scenario assumes that land-use controls restricting the use of groundwater will be implemented for the properties within Exposure Unit #1 and Exposure Unit #2 where groundwater impacts are present. The RC used for the groundwater source was conservatively obtained from the maximum groundwater contaminant concentration detected in MW-4, which is the closest monitoring well to the POE. The RC used for the soil source was the maximum concentration detected at the site (GP-2). The modeling results for the protection of groundwater use did not indicate an exceedance of the Site-Specific Target Levels (SSTLs), and groundwater sampling completed at the site confirms the groundwater plume is stable.

For the protection of surface water use evaluation, the POE was determined to be the closest point of an unnamed tributary of Long Branch, located approximately 225 feet downgradient of monitoring well MW-4. The modeling results for the protection of surface water use did not indicate exceedances of the SSTLs, and groundwater sampling completed at the site confirms the groundwater plume is stable.

Based on the results of this risk assessment, H&H concludes that the release at the subject site does not pose an unacceptable risk and recommends site closure in accordance with the DSCA Program’s risk-based rules. Land-use controls restricting the use of groundwater will be implemented on the source property and the adjacent commercial property to the south.

## 4.0 RMP Components

### 4.1 Summary of Prior Assessment and Interim Actions

The former Galaxy Cleaners facility operated in the Waverly Place Shopping Center from the mid-1990s to mid-2010. The shopping center property is composed of a 22.54-acre parcel containing a multi-tenant shopping center building, which was built in 1987. The former dry-cleaning facility operated in the southern portion of the building in a tenant space that is now part of the Whole Foods grocery store. The former dry-cleaning operations involved the use of PCE until July 2000 when the facility reportedly switched to a petroleum dry-cleaning solvent.

In 2004, a Limited Soil and Groundwater Assessment Report completed by Professional Service Industries, Inc. (PSI) evaluated a potential release of dry-cleaning solvents. As part of this assessment, five soil borings were advanced including two samples within the dry-cleaning facility and three samples around the outside of the dry cleaning facility (soil boring IDs: GP-1, GP-2, GP-3, GP-4, GP-5). PCE (0.0064 mg/kg) was only detected in GP-2 collected at a depth of 18-20 feet below ground surface (bgs). The detected PCE concentration is below the DSCA Tier I Risk Based Screening Level (RBSL) of 0.023 mg/kg. One groundwater sample was collected from soil boring GP-5. PCE was detected in GP-5 groundwater sample at a concentration of 0.0018 mg/L, which exceeds the 2L Standard of 0.0007 mg/L. In 2005, the site was enrolled in the DSCA Program.

In July 2005, Metcalf & Eddy, Inc. completed a Prioritization Assessment Report which documented a receptor survey, advancement of three soil borings (SB-1, SB-2, and SB-3), and the installation and sampling of three monitoring wells. No dry-cleaning related constituents were detected in the soil samples. PCE was detected in groundwater samples collected from MW-1 (0.0018 mg/L) and MW-3 (0.0053 mg/L) at concentrations above the 2L Standard of 0.0007 mg/L for PCE. Methyl tert-butyl ether (MTBE) was also detected in the groundwater sample collected from monitoring well MW-3 (0.0063 mg/L) at a concentration below the 2L Standard of 0.02 mg/L for MTBE.

Withers & Ravenel (W&R) installed and sampled four additional groundwater monitoring wells (MW-4 through MW-7) and collected samples from the existing three monitoring wells in January 2007. Consistent with the 2005 data, PCE was detected in the groundwater samples collected from MW-1 (0.0012 mg/L) and MW-3 (0.0052 mg/L) and MTBE was detected in MW-3 (0.0052 mg/L). No dry-cleaning related constituents were detected in MW-2, or MW-4 through MW-7.

W&R completed quarterly groundwater sampling of the site monitoring wells between November 2007 and November 2008. The results of the quarterly sampling events were similar to the previous January 2007 event. In January and February 2008, W&R conducted additional soil assessment activities inside the dry-cleaning facility which included the advancement of seven soil borings (WR-1 through WR-7) with soil samples collected from multiple depths within each boring. There were no dry-cleaning related constituents detected in the soil samples.

Vapor intrusion assessment activities were conducted at the site by W&R between 2010 and 2011. Initially, W&R collected a sub-slab vapor sample within the former dry-cleaning facility in February 2010. PCE was detected at a concentration of 328 µg/m³. W&R collected a second sub slab-vapor sample and an indoor air sample in June 2011 after dry-cleaning operations had ceased and the dry-cleaning machinery had been removed. The sub-slab vapor sample contained a PCE concentration of 18 µg/m³, and the indoor air sample contained detectable concentrations of PCE (16 µg/m³), trichloroethene (TCE, 9.1 µg/m³) and cis 1,2 dichloroethene (0.1 µg/m³). Given the relatively low sub-slab PCE concentrations and lack of TCE in the sub-slab vapor, the detected indoor air concentrations were not believed to be associated with vapor intrusion.

In August 2012, W&R collected one indoor air sample and one sub-slab vapor sample inside the former Galaxy Cleaners facility. No dry-cleaning constituents were detected in the indoor air sample, and the sub-slab vapor sample only exhibited a low concentration of PCE (58 µg/m³). As documented in the site risk assessment, no unacceptable vapor intrusion risks were identified at the site based on the assessment results.

In 2013, H&H collected samples from monitoring wells MW-4 through MW-7 to evaluate current concentrations prior to closure. Monitoring wells MW-1 and MW-2 were dry at the time at sampling, and MW-3 could not be sampled due to insufficient water. PCE was detected in the groundwater sample from well MW-4 (0.00229 mg/L), and MTBE was detected in in the groundwater sample from well MW-6 (0.00118 mg/L). No other constituents were detected in the groundwater samples.

In May 2014, H&H completed limited additional groundwater assessment activities which included the installation of a nested pair of monitoring wells, MW-8S and MW-8I, in the source area to delineate the vertical extent of PCE in groundwater. Subsequently, groundwater samples were collected from existing wells MW-4, MW-5, MW-7, and the newly installed wells MW-8S and MW-8I. PCE was detected in the groundwater sample from well MW-4 (0.00105 mg/L). No other dry-cleaning related constituents were detected in the May 2014 groundwater samples. H&H concluded that based on recent and historical groundwater data, the groundwater plume associated with Galaxy Cleaners appears to be adequately defined and stable. H&H completed a risk assessment in April 2015 which indicated that the risk associated with the site contamination is below the risk levels acceptable by the DSCA Program. If land-use controls are implemented to ensure the risk assessment assumptions remain valid in the future, no further action status is recommended for the site.

### 4.2 Remedial Action

According to the DSCA Program’s RBCA guidance, no remedial action is necessary if four site conditions are met. Each of these conditions and their applicability to the subject site are addressed below.

**Condition 1: The dissolved plume is stable or decreasing.**  
As discussed in Section 4.1, quarterly groundwater monitoring was conducted between November 2007 and November 2008, during which groundwater samples were collected from the seven on-site monitoring wells. Additional groundwater monitoring was completed in 2013 and 2014. The only volatile organic compounds detected during the groundwater sampling events were PCE, acetone, MTBE, chloroform, and bromodichloromethane. Acetone, MTBE, chloroform, and bromodichloromethane are not compounds associated with dry-cleaning operations. The detected concentrations of acetone, MTBE, and chloroform are below their respective 2L Standards of 6 mg/L, 0.02 mg/L, and 0.07 mg/L, respectively. Bromodichloromethane was detected above the 2L Standard of 0.0006 mg/L; however, this constituent is not associated with dry-cleaning solvents and can be found in municipal water as a disinfection byproduct. PCE is the only dry-cleaning related constituent detected in site groundwater.

H&H prepared PCE concentration versus time graphs for the sampling events completed at the site. As shown in the graphs, consistently low concentrations of PCE have been detected at the site with concentrations either decreasing or slightly fluctuating over time. Based on the site data, H&H concludes that the plume is stable. Documentation of the plume stability evaluation, including a figure showing the monitoring well locations, a table showing historical groundwater analytical data, and PCE concentration versus time graphs, is included in Appendix A.

**Condition 2: The maximum concentration within the exposure domain for every complete exposure pathway of any constituent of concern (COC) is less than ten times the RC of that COC.**  
For the risk assessment, H&H used the maximum concentrations detected at the site as the RC for each constituent. Thus, this condition has been met for all COCs and exposure pathways.

**Condition 3: Adequate assurance is provided that the land-use assumptions used in the DSCA Program’s RBCA process are not violated for current or future conditions.**  
As discussed in Section 6.0, land-use controls will be implemented for the source property and adjacent non-source property to ensure that the assumptions made in the risk assessment remain valid in the future.

**Condition 4: There are no ecological concerns at the site.**  
W&R completed a Level 1 Ecological Risk Assessment dated May 2007 for the site in accordance with the DSCA Program’s RBCA guidance. The results of the evaluation indicate that the release does not pose an unacceptable ecological risk. The completed Level 1 Ecological Risk Assessment Checklists A and B and associated attachments are included as Appendix B.

The site’s compliance with the four above referenced conditions confirms that the contaminant concentrations are not likely to pose an unacceptable risk either at present or in the future. Existing groundwater contamination is at low concentrations and is expected to naturally attenuate over time. The appropriate remedial action is to implement land-use controls on the source property and adjacent off-source property where groundwater contamination is present.

No further sampling or other data collection activities are proposed for the site. As such, this section is not applicable.

## 6.0 Land-Use Restrictions

As discussed in Section 3.0, the risk assessment for the Galaxy Cleaners site was based on the following assumption:

- Groundwater will not be utilized on the source property and one off-source property

Institutional controls will be implemented for the source property to ensure that groundwater will not be utilized. A notice will be placed on the deed for the off-source impacted property identifying the presence of groundwater contamination and citing regulations prohibiting the installation of a water supply well in a contaminated aquifer. Notices of Dry-Cleaning Solvent Remediation (NDCSRs) were prepared for the source property and adjacent off-source property. The NDCSR for the source property is included in Appendix C, and the NDCSR for the off-source property is included in Appendix D. A plat showing the locations and types of dry-cleaning solvent contamination is included as an exhibit to each NDCSR. The locations of dry-cleaning solvent contamination are the areas where contaminants have been detected above unrestrictive use standards.

## 7.0 Long-Term Stewardship Plan

The NDCSR for the source property contains a clause which requires the owner of the former Galaxy Cleaners source property to submit a notarized “Annual Certification of Land-Use Restrictions” to North Carolina Department of Environmental Quality (NCDEQ) on an annual basis certifying that the NDCSR remains recorded with the Register of Deeds and that land-use restrictions are being complied with. An example of such a notice is included in Appendix E.

## 8.0 RMP Implementation Schedule

Since the groundwater plume is stable and possible exposure to the contamination is managed through the NDCSRs, no additional site remediation activities are required to implement the RMP. A 30-day public comment period will be held to allow the community an opportunity to comment on the proposed strategy. Appendix F includes example documents that will be used to announce the public comment period in the local newspaper and to inform local officials, nearby property owners, and interested parties. Upon completion of the public comment period and final approval of the RMP, the NDCSRs will be filed with the Wake County Register of Deeds and will complete the RMP schedule.

## 9.0 Criteria for Demonstrating RMP Success

The RMP will be successfully implemented once the required NDCSRs have been executed and recorded with the Wake County Register of Deeds. The NDCSR for each property may, at the request of the owner of the property, be canceled by NCDEQ after the risk to public health and the environment associated with the dry-cleaning solvent contamination and any other contaminants included in the dry-cleaning solvent assessment and remediation agreement has been eliminated as a result of remediation of the property. If NCDEQ is notified of a change in site conditions, per the notification requirements detailed in the NDCSR, the RMP will be reviewed to determine if the site conditions have impacted the requirements set forth in the NDCSR and if changes are required. Enforcement of the RMP will be maintained through receipt of the “Annual Certification of Land-Use Restrictions” from the property owner as part of the NDCSR requirements.

## 10.0 Contingency Plan if RMP Fails

As discussed above, unless the DSCA Program is notified of a change in land-use conditions at the site, per the notification requirements detailed in this plan, the RMP will remain in effect until the RMP has met its objectives and is considered a success. Pursuant to N.C.G.S. 143-215.104K, if any of the land-use restrictions (LURs) set out in the NDCSR are violated, the owner of the site property at the time the LURs are violated, the owner’s successors and assigns, and the owner’s agents who directed or contracted for alteration of the site in violation of the LURs, shall be held liable for the remediation of all contaminants to unrestricted use standards.

## 11.0 Conclusions and Recommendations

H&H has prepared this RMP for the Galaxy Cleaners site on behalf of the DSCA Program. The results of the risk assessment indicated that contaminant concentrations at the site do not pose an unacceptable risk. The groundwater contaminant plume associated with the site appears to be stable or decreasing. This RMP specifies that the NDCSR requirements provide notification that land-use conditions observed during the risk assessment evaluation remain valid in the future. Based on the documentation contained in this report, H&H recommends issuance of a “No Further Action” letter.

This Notice has been approved by the North Carolina Department of Environmental Quality, or its successor in function (hereinafter “DEQ”) under the authority of the Dry-Cleaning Solvent Cleanup Act of 1997, as amended, N.C.G.S. § 143-215.104A *et seq.* (hereinafter “DSCA”), and is required to be filed in the Register of Deeds' Office in the county or counties in which the land is located, pursuant to NCGS § 143-215.104I. A Notice will be recorded separately in each chain of title of the Contamination Site.

Groundwater under the Property is contaminated with dry-cleaning solvents associated with dry-cleaning operations at the former Galaxy Cleaners (DSCA Site DC920029) located at 101-B New Waverly Place, Cary, in the Waverly Place Shopping Center. **A risk assessment of the contaminated property concluded that the contamination poses no unacceptable risk as long as groundwater on the property is not used as a source of water for any water supply wells.**

Pursuant to N.C.G.S. § 143-215.104I, this Notice is being filed in order to reduce or eliminate the danger to public health or the environment posed by the Property. Attached hereto as **Exhibit A** is a reduction, to 8 1/2" x 11", of the survey plat component of the Notice required by N.C.G.S. § 143-215.104M. The survey plat has been prepared and certified by a professional land surveyor and meets the requirements of G.S. 47-30, and contains the following information required by N.C.G.S. § 143-215.104M:

(1) A description of the location and dimensions of the areas of potential environmental concern with respect to permanently surveyed benchmarks; and 
(2) The type, location and quantity of regulated dry-cleaning solvent contamination and other contaminants known to exist on the Property. 

Attached hereto as **Exhibit B** is a legal description of the Property that would be sufficient as a description in an instrument of conveyance.

#### USE OF GROUNDWATER PROHIBITED BY STATE AND LOCAL REGULATIONS

Groundwater on this property contains contaminants that exceed unrestricted use standards. Pursuant to 15A North Carolina Administrative Code 02C .0107(b)(1), “(t)he source of water for any water supply well shall not be from a water bearing zone or aquifer that is contaminated.” Therefore, state law prohibits construction of a water supply well on this property unless it can be demonstrated that the water pumped from the well is not contaminated. Further, pursuant to North Carolina General Statute 87-88(c) and 15A North Carolina Administrative Code 02C .0112(a), no well may be constructed or maintained in a manner whereby it could be a source or channel of contamination of the groundwater supply or any aquifer.

#### FUTURE SALES, LEASES, CONVEYANCES AND TRANSFERS

When any portion of the Property is sold, leased, conveyed or transferred, pursuant to NCGS § 143-215.104M the deed or other instrument of transfer shall contain in the description section, in no smaller type than that used in the body of the deed or instrument, a statement that the Property has been contaminated with dry-cleaning solvent and, if appropriate, cleaned up under the DSCA.

#### CANCELLATION OF THE NOTICE

The Notice may, at the request of the Property Owner, be canceled by DEQ after the risk to public health and the environment associated with the dry-cleaning solvent contamination and any other contaminants included in the DSCA Remediation Agreement have been eliminated as a result of remediation of the Property to unrestricted use standards.
